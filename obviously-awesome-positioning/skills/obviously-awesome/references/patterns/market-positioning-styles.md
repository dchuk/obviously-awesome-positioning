# Market Positioning Styles

## Problem Framing

You've done the hard work of identifying your [competitive alternatives](../topics/competitive-alternatives.md), [unique attributes](../topics/unique-attributes.md), and [value themes](../topics/value-themes.md). Now you face a deceptively simple question: *what market are you in?*

This is where many products go wrong. The market category you inhabited when the product was first conceived may no longer be the right one. Meanwhile, every choice of market frame triggers a set of assumptions in prospects' minds — about who you compete with, what features they should expect, and what price is reasonable. Choose the wrong frame and your strengths become invisible. Choose the right one and customers arrive pre-convinced.

The pain is real: your differentiators cannot overcome the market leader's advantages in every sales conversation. Prospects keep asking, "How are you better than [leader]?" and you keep losing on the leader's terms. That's a positioning frame problem, not a product problem.

Step 8 of the positioning process resolves this: **find a market frame of reference that puts your strengths at the center**, then choose the right style for competing within it. The three styles are **Head to Head**, **Big Fish Small Pond**, and **Create a New Game**.

---

## How the Market Frame Works

> "The market category you choose gives prospects clues about what to compare you with, what features to expect, what price to anticipate, and what benefits to look for." _source: ch11 opening paragraphs_

Choosing a market frame of reference is not a branding exercise — it is a deliberate act of context-setting. The goal is to trigger assumptions that work *to your advantage*. The frame must already exist in the minds of customers (unless you are deliberately creating a new market with the Create a New Game style).

One practical method for identifying candidate frames is **abductive reasoning**: isolate your key features and their value, then ask what types of products typically have those features or deliver that value. Start with what you can provably do, and work backward to which market category that evidence most naturally fits.

A caution: be extremely careful when seeking feedback from customers about what market you are in. Customers are not positioning experts. They default to the most obvious category connected to their industry or job function — which is often not the best frame for your strengths. _source: ch11 §Ask your customers (but be cautious)_

Similarly, adjacent markets deserve examination — particularly when your product has evolved or when market lines are shifting due to new companies, technologies, or ideas. But only move into a growing adjacent market if your strengths are obviously at home there. Simply wanting the buzz of a hot market is insufficient justification.

## Style 1: Head to Head

**Head to Head positioning** is a style in which you compete directly against established players in a well-defined market, aiming to be the leader of the entire category by winning on the currently accepted purchase criteria.

### When it fits

Use Head to Head only if you are already the market leader, or if no clear leader yet exists in an otherwise understood category. A large, well-resourced company may also attempt it when market conditions — new technology, regulatory changes, economic shifts — are moving against the incumbent leader.

### When it doesn't fit

Do not use Head to Head if you are a small startup launching into a market with an established leader. If you cannot credibly demonstrate superiority on the currently accepted purchase criteria, or if you lack the features, customer proof points, or brand required to compete on the leader's terms, Head to Head is a trap.

### Decision rule

| Condition | Answer |
|---|---|
| You are already the market leader | ✅ Use Head to Head |
| The category is understood but no leader exists yet | ✅ Use Head to Head |
| You are large with clear advantage and market is shifting against incumbent | ✅ Consider Head to Head |
| You are a startup entering a market with an established leader | ❌ Do not use Head to Head |
| You cannot beat the leader on the accepted purchase criteria | ❌ Do not use Head to Head |

## Style 2: Big Fish, Small Pond

**Big Fish, Small Pond positioning** is a style in which you carve off a subsegment of an existing market where the rules differ just enough to give your product an edge over the category leader, targeting buyers with needs not being met by that leader.

### The key insight

> "Smaller subsegments are easier to reach, word-of-mouth spreads faster, and success with one customer is directly applicable to the next. You are simply unselecting buyers who were never going to buy anyway." _source: ch11 §Big Fish, Small Pond_

Dominating a small, well-defined subsegment is generally much easier than attacking a larger market — and focusing on a niche does not prevent future growth.

### Subsegmenting

**Subsegmenting** is the process of splitting an existing market into smaller, more homogeneous pieces — by industry, geography, company size, or any axis where your product has a distinct advantage. The subsegment must have a clearly definable, important, and *provably unmet* need — one the market leader structurally cannot or will not address.

When executing Big Fish, Small Pond, you must still demonstrate that you meet or exceed the existing category's basic purchase criteria. You are capturing a slice of the market, not redefining it.

### When it fits

Use Big Fish, Small Pond when:
- The broader category is well-defined and understood by buyers
- A clear market leader exists and you cannot take them on across the full market
- Identifiable subsegments have distinct, important needs not being met by the leader
- You can construct a list of prospective buyers in the subsegment through research or data
- Your competitive advantage is difficult for the leader to copy
- The subsegment is large enough to meet your near-term revenue goals

### When it doesn't fit

Do not use Big Fish, Small Pond when the broader category is not yet well understood — subsegmenting a confused market creates more confusion, not clarity. Also avoid it when no leader exists yet (Head to Head is more appropriate), when the subsegment's needs are trivial or cosmetic, or when the subsegment is too small to support your revenue targets.

### Decision rule

| Condition | Answer |
|---|---|
| Broader category is well-defined with a clear leader | ✅ Prerequisite met |
| Subsegment has a distinct, important, provably unmet need | ✅ Prerequisite met |
| You can list prospective buyers in the subsegment | ✅ Prerequisite met |
| Subsegment is large enough for near-term revenue goals | ✅ Prerequisite met |
| Broader category is not well understood | ❌ Do not use Big Fish, Small Pond |
| Subsegment need is cosmetic or trivially copyable | ❌ Do not use Big Fish, Small Pond |

## Style 3: Create a New Game

**Create a New Game positioning** is a style in which you define an entirely new market category, prove it deserves to exist, shape its purchase criteria to your strengths, and establish yourself as its leader.

### The real work

Category creation is hard, slow, expensive work. The first task is not selling your product — it is selling the market on the *problem*. If the category doesn't already exist, customers are not yet aware they have a problem and do not understand the cost of not solving it. Awareness must precede solution consideration. _source: ch11 §The work of Create a New Game_

Three additional requirements must all be true for this style to be credible:

1. **Demonstrable difference.** Your product must be demonstrably and inarguably new and different from what exists in other market categories. Wishful thinking won't convince prospects; the distinctiveness needs to be obviously true in the minds of customers, not just asserted by you.
2. **Compelling 'Why now?'** Timing is critical. There must be a convincing answer grounded in new technology, buyer behavior shifts, or environmental changes. Without this, the case for the new category will not be credible.
3. **Resources for the long game.** A fast follower can enter the space as soon as buyers start to understand the category you've built. You need deep-pocketed, patient investors and a plan to defend leadership at every stage.

### When it fits

Use Create a New Game only after evaluating every possible existing market category and concluding none can showcase your true differentiators — or if your company is large and powerful enough to educate the market. It also applies when your solution requires both new category boundaries and new purchase criteria simultaneously.

### When it doesn't fit

Do not use Create a New Game if an existing market category can adequately position your strengths. Do not use it if you lack the resources and time for a long-term market education campaign, cannot answer 'Why now?' with compelling external market forces, or are a small startup without patient investors willing to fund years of category-building.

### Decision rule

| Condition | Answer |
|---|---|
| No existing category showcases your true differentiators | ✅ Consider Create a New Game |
| Solution requires new category boundaries AND new purchase criteria | ✅ Consider Create a New Game |
| Massive enabling change exists (tech, behavior, regulation) | ✅ Prerequisite met |
| Deep-pocketed patient investors are committed | ✅ Prerequisite met |
| An existing category can adequately position your strengths | ❌ Do not use Create a New Game |
| You cannot answer 'Why now?' credibly | ❌ Do not use Create a New Game |
| You lack resources for a multi-year education campaign | ❌ Do not use Create a New Game |

---

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use this step (Step 8) whenever:**
- You are launching a new product or entering a new market
- Your product has evolved significantly since the original category was chosen
- Prospects consistently compare you unfavorably to a dominant leader you cannot credibly beat
- Your differentiators fail to land in current sales conversations

**Do not skip or shortcut this step when:**
- The market category was chosen at product conception and has never been revisited
- You are tempted to move into an adjacent market purely because it is growing and buzzy — only move if your strengths are obviously at home there

## Code Examples

The following examples are structured as decision prompts you can run through for any product. Use them as a checklist before committing to a style.

```
## Head to Head Checklist
- [ ] We are the current market leader, OR the category has no clear leader yet
- [ ] We can demonstrate superiority on the currently accepted purchase criteria
- [ ] We have the features, customer proof points, and brand to compete on the leader's terms
- [ ] Market conditions (tech shift, regulation, economic change) are moving against the incumbent [required only if we are a large challenger]

## Big Fish, Small Pond Checklist
- [ ] The broader category is well understood by buyers
- [ ] A clear market leader exists
- [ ] We have identified a subsegment with a distinct, important, provably unmet need
- [ ] We can construct a list of prospective buyers in that subsegment
- [ ] Our competitive advantage in the subsegment is difficult for the leader to copy
- [ ] The subsegment is large enough for our near-term revenue goals
- [ ] We still meet or exceed the category's basic purchase criteria

## Create a New Game Checklist
- [ ] We have evaluated every possible existing category — none showcases our true differentiators
- [ ] Our product is demonstrably and inarguably different from anything in existing categories
- [ ] We can answer 'Why now?' with a specific, external enabling change
- [ ] We have deep-pocketed, patient investors committed to a multi-year campaign
- [ ] Our go-to-market plan sells the problem first, then the solution
```

## Anti-Patterns

**Defaulting to the Original Market Category** — Staying in the market category baked into the product's original conception, even after the product and market have evolved. This feels natural or internally obvious but leaves your differentiators invisible. *Why harmful:* prospects constantly compare you unfavorably to a dominant leader you cannot credibly beat, and your strengths never get a fair hearing. Fix: deliberately re-evaluate using abductive reasoning, adjacent market analysis, or cautious customer feedback. _source: ch11 opening paragraphs_

**Small Company Competing Head to Head Against an Established Leader** — A small or new company positioning itself to win the *entire* market, directly taking on a dominant incumbent without a decisive competitive advantage. *Why harmful:* the first question from every prospect becomes "How are you better than [market leader]?" — and you consistently lose on the leader's terms. Your product gets described as a "cheaper, crappier" version. Fix: switch to Big Fish, Small Pond and identify a subsegment the incumbent cannot easily follow you into. _source: ch11 §When to use the Head to Head style_

**Subsegmenting Without a Genuine Unmet Need** — Targeting a subsegment defined by superficial characteristics (demographic labels, cosmetic branding differences) rather than a truly distinct and important unmet need. *Why harmful:* customers in the subsegment show no reaction or confusion when you describe the special value; the market leader can trivially add a feature to neutralize your position. Fix: validate that the unmet need is something the leader structurally cannot or will not address. _source: ch11 §When to use the Big Fish, Small Pond style_

**Premature or Under-Resourced Category Creation** — Attempting to create a new market category without the resources, time, or market conditions needed to sustain the long-term educational effort. *Why harmful:* a well-funded fast follower enters the space just as buyers start to understand the category you built, harvesting the market you created. Fix: either find an existing category, or secure deep-pocketed patient investors and commit to defending leadership at every stage. _source: ch11 §When to use the Create a New Game style_
