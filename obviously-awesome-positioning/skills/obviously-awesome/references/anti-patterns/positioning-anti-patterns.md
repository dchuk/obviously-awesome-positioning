# Positioning Anti-Patterns

Every positioning mistake has the same root: someone skipped a step, accepted the default, or let momentum substitute for deliberate choice. This catalog names each anti-pattern with its canonical label, explains why it is harmful, lists the signals that surface it, and points to the fix. Use it to audit your own positioning work or to diagnose a product that isn't resonating.

---

## The Problem These Patterns Solve

You've launched, you're spending on marketing, and prospects still can't explain what your product does. Or customers love it but new deals are slow and painful. Or your roadmap is driven by churned customers who bought for the wrong reasons. These are not budget problems or sales problems — they are positioning problems. Weak positioning compounds upstream into every downstream tactic: ads, decks, demos, pricing, and roadmap.

The anti-patterns below are the specific failure modes that produce these symptoms. Naming them gives your team a shared language for diagnosing and fixing them before they compound further.

---

## Category 1: Foundational Mistakes

These errors occur before any process starts. They are beliefs and defaults that prevent good positioning from ever being attempted.

### **Throwing Budget at Weak Positioning**

Pouring a larger marketing budget behind confusing, unclear positioning in hopes that more spend will compensate for the messaging problem.

**Why harmful.** More impressions of a confusing message produce more confused prospects, not more pipeline. Spend scales the problem, not the solution.

**Signals.**
- Prospects still can't explain what your product does after seeing ads
- Increasing spend does not improve close rates or pipeline quality

**Fix.** Fix the positioning first so the message is clear and compelling, then scale marketing spend. _source: ch01 §'Like speaking Japanese slowly and loudly'_

---

### **Letting the Market Position You by Default**

Failing to deliberately set a position, so prospects invent one — often one that hides key strengths or misrepresents the product's value.

**Why harmful.** The position prospects invent is based on the closest familiar thing, which is rarely the frame that makes your product look best.

**Signals.**
- Current customers love the product but new prospects can't figure out what is being sold
- Sales team spends more time explaining what the product is than closing deals

**Fix.** Proactively and deliberately define how your product is the best at something that a defined market cares about. _source: ch01 §'If your prospects can't figure out what you do'_

---

### **Building Features for Churned / Misfit Customers**

Development teams spend time building features demanded by customers who bought for the wrong reasons — due to weak positioning attracting the wrong buyers — while neglecting happiest and best-fit customers.

**Why harmful.** You end up with a roadmap driven by disappointed customers rather than ideal ones, compounding the positioning problem in the product itself.

**Signals.**
- High customer churn shortly after purchase
- New customers constantly requesting features with no plan to deliver
- Development roadmap driven by disappointed customers rather than ideal ones

**Fix.** Improve positioning to attract best-fit customers from the start, reducing post-sale feature pressure from misfit customers. _source: ch01 §'You have high customer churn'_

## Category 2: Context and Category Mistakes

These errors involve choosing the wrong frame of reference — either by staying too attached to the original intent or by picking a category whose default assumptions work against you.

### **Trap 1 — Stuck on Original Intent**

Product creators remain attached to the category they originally set out to build, failing to recognize that iterative development has transformed the product into something else.

**Why harmful.** Customers encounter a mismatch between positioning and reality, leading to confusion or dismissal. The product's real strengths are buried under the wrong framing.

**Signals.**
- Customers are confused by the product or say it doesn't match the pitch
- Differentiating features feel 'wrong' or out of place within the stated category
- The product's strongest capabilities are treated as secondary

**Fix.** Regularly evaluate what the product has actually become — not what it was planned to be — and reposition it to match its real strengths and the category that best surfaces its distinct value. _source: ch02 §'Trap 1'_

---

### **Trap 2 — Stale Positioning in a Shifted Market**

A product that was well positioned for a market retains that positioning even after the market's language, preferences, and competitive landscape have evolved, making the offering appear outdated or irrelevant.

**Why harmful.** Former customers migrate to competitors whose product is nearly identical but whose positioning speaks to current buyer language. You lose deals not on features but on perception.

**Signals.**
- Former customers are migrating to competitors whose product is nearly identical
- The category label used in positioning feels dated to target buyers
- New buyer segments exist who would value the product but don't self-identify with the current positioning

**Fix.** Continuously monitor market shifts in customer language, competitor positioning, and buying preferences; update positioning terminology and category to reflect current market context. _source: ch02 §'Trap 2'_

---

### **Frankencake — Forcing Features into the Wrong Category**

Positioning a product as a member of a category where its most distinctive features appear unnatural or wrong — 'cake on a stick' — causing prospects to reject it because it seems like a poorly conceived version of the default category rather than a superior version of its true category.

**Why harmful.** Key differentiators sound like defects or oddities. The product loses head-to-head comparisons against category incumbents despite having a genuinely superior capability. Judges or evaluators prefer standard alternatives over the product in its current framing.

**Signals.**
- Key differentiators sound like defects or oddities when described within the current category
- The product loses head-to-head comparisons against category incumbents despite having a genuinely superior capability

**Fix.** Identify the category in which the product's unique features are natural and expected — not exceptions — and reframe the product within that category (e.g., 'cake pop' instead of 'cake on a stick'). _source: ch02 §'cake pop example'_

---

### **Choosing the Wrong Market Category**

Positioning a product in a market category that triggers assumptions that do not apply to the product, forcing marketing and sales to spend effort battling those incorrect assumptions instead of selling value.

**Why harmful.** Every sales conversation starts with correcting false expectations rather than building value. The category label imports baggage that actively harms you.

**Signals.**
- Prospects consistently ask for features the product does not have
- Customers love the product but prospects can't figure it out
- Key unique features are not expected or valued in the declared market category

**Fix.** Audit the assumptions triggered by your current market category label; reposition in the category whose assumptions best align with your product's actual strengths and unique features. _source: ch03 §Positioning Story: An email-for-lawyers solution that isn't really email_

---

### **Positioning Into a Category That Forces Wrong Comparisons**

Placing a product in a familiar market category even though the product's core strengths are fundamentally different from others in that category.

**Why harmful.** Customers compare the product against alternatives it does not actually resemble, obscuring the product's real value.

**Signals.**
- Sales conversations spend most of their time explaining why the product is NOT like other products in its stated category
- Customers are left confused about how the product fits their existing assumptions
- The product's most valuable capabilities are not associated with its named category

**Fix.** Identify the market or metaphor that makes the product's unique strengths central and obvious. Reposition into a different category — even a newly created one — where the product clearly wins on its own terms. _source: ch06 §Clearpath Robotics positioning story_

## Category 3: Process Mistakes

These errors occur during the positioning exercise itself — wrong starting point, wrong tool, or wrong inputs.

### **Using the Traditional Positioning Statement**

Filling in a FOR/IS A/WHICH PROVIDES/UNLIKE template to 'do' positioning.

**Why harmful.** This assumes you already know the best answers, reinforces status quo thinking, produces output that is hard to remember and that teams never actually use, and gives no guidance on whether positioning is good or what to do next.

**Signals.**
- Teams complete the exercise and file it away, never referring back to it
- Marketing, product, and sales teams cannot recall or use the output
- Positioning defaults to the most obvious 'status quo' framing of the product
- No creative alternatives to the current market framing are ever considered

**Fix.** Replace the positioning statement exercise with the Five (Plus One) Components framework, working through competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value, target market characteristics, market category, and relevant trends in order. _source: ch03 §Why You Should Never Create a Positioning Statement_

---

### **Status Quo / Default Positioning**

Believing there is only one possible 'default' way to position a product and never questioning it, leaving the company blind to potential shifts and giving existing market leaders a structural advantage.

**Why harmful.** You cede positioning strategy to whoever defined your category first, usually a market leader whose framing was designed to favor them.

**Signals.**
- Team has never seriously considered positioning the product differently
- Positioning mirrors exactly how the market leader is described
- No discussion of whether there is a better market category or frame

**Fix.** Explicitly work through the five components framework to surface alternative ways the product could be framed, rather than accepting the first obvious category. _source: ch03 §Why You Should Never Create a Positioning Statement_

---

### **Defining Features Before Competitive Alternatives**

Starting the positioning process by listing key features or value without first establishing what customers see as the competitive alternative.

**Why harmful.** Positioning that doesn't connect with how customers actually evaluate solutions fails to resonate even if every feature claim is true.

**Signals.**
- Positioning work starts with a feature list or value proposition brainstorm
- Team has not researched what customers currently use instead of the product
- Claimed differentiators are not actually unique versus the real alternatives customers consider

**Fix.** Always begin with competitive alternatives; only then identify which of your attributes are truly unique relative to those alternatives. _source: ch03 §Each component has a relationship to the others_

---

### **Feature-First Positioning**

Starting the positioning process by laying out a product's unique features rather than starting with customers.

**Why harmful.** This unconsciously anchors you to a competitor frame that you define, which frequently differs from how customers actually see competition.

**Signals.**
- Positioning is defined by enumerating differentiating features before identifying target customers
- Competitors referenced are rival startups competing for investment, not alternatives customers actually consider

**Fix.** Start with a list of best-fit customers. Use their frame of reference — how the product compares to what they use today — as the foundation of positioning. _source: ch04 §'But I want to start with my product, not my customers!'_

---

### **Using Investor Positioning for Customers**

Applying the same 'disruption' or future-vision narrative used for investors to customer-facing materials.

**Why harmful.** Customers hear 'disruption' as replacing things they have invested in, which can be confusing or terrifying rather than compelling.

**Signals.**
- Website or sales deck uses language like 'disruption,' 'market leader in five years,' or addresses future growth plans
- Sales deck and investor pitch deck tell the same story

**Fix.** Create separate positioning for customers focused on immediate problems and immediate value; reserve vision and disruption language for investor contexts. _source: ch04 §'Shouldn't we position our product for customers the same way we position it for investors?'_

---

### **Surveying the Entire Customer Base for Positioning Signals**

Surveying all customers — happy and less happy alike — to understand why they love the product.

**Why harmful.** Results are muddy with no clear patterns. Mediocre satisfaction signals drown out the strong signal from your best-fit customers.

**Signals.**
- Survey results show no consistent pattern in why customers chose the product
- Analysis includes moderately satisfied customers alongside highly satisfied ones

**Fix.** Filter to only the ecstatic, best-fit customers before analyzing patterns in satisfaction and preference. _source: ch04 §'Looking back at the first few times I repositioned a product'_

---

### **Positioning Exercise Without Business Leader**

Running a positioning exercise that is not driven by the business leader — e.g., delegating the effort entirely to marketing.

**Why harmful.** Positioning that the CEO or division leader didn't help build is positioning the organization fails to adopt.

**Signals.**
- Marketing department is solely 'owning' positioning
- Business leader is absent from positioning discussions
- Positioning outputs are not being adopted across functions

**Fix.** Ensure the CEO, founder, or relevant division/business unit leader is present, visible, and driving the positioning effort from the start. _source: ch05 §Who should be there?_

---

### **Overstaffed Positioning Meeting**

Having too many people in the positioning meeting.

**Why harmful.** Hard to hear from each group; one or two groups dominate discussion and quieter functions cannot get their perspectives heard.

**Signals.**
- More than two representatives from any single function
- Certain groups consistently dominate discussion

**Fix.** Limit representation to one or at most two senior people per functional group. _source: ch05 §Although there is great value in having representation_

---

### **Carrying Positioning Baggage Into New Positioning Work**

Allowing the product's historical framing — its original intended market, early terminology, and founding assumptions — to unconsciously constrain positioning discussions.

**Why harmful.** The team cannot see better alternatives because the current framing is treated as fact rather than as one option among many.

**Signals.**
- Current market category is the same one imagined when the product was conceived
- Terminology used to describe features hasn't changed since launch
- Founders or long-tenured employees dominate positioning discussions with historical rationale

**Fix.** Explicitly surface where the product's history appears in current positioning, acknowledge it as baggage, and get team agreement to suspend current positioning opinions for the duration of the exercise. _source: ch06 §'Let go of your positioning baggage'_

## Category 4: Competitive Alternatives Mistakes

These errors occur in Step 4, where the goal is to list what best-fit customers would actually use instead of your product.

### **Product-Centric Competitor View**

Starting from the product and its features to determine competitors, rather than asking what customers would actually use as a replacement.

**Why harmful.** Your competitor list reflects your expert knowledge, not your customer's mental model. You optimize against the wrong alternatives.

**Signals.**
- Competitor list consists only of products that look like direct feature competitors
- Team is surprised when customers name non-obvious alternatives

**Fix.** Ask best-fit customers what they would do if your product didn't exist, and use their answers to build the competitive alternatives list. _source: ch07 opening paragraphs_

---

### **Problem-Only Customer Research**

Asking customers only about the problems they are trying to solve, without asking about alternatives.

**Why harmful.** Customers are often poor at describing problems with enough nuance, and problem-focused answers do not reveal the competitive category customers place you in.

**Signals.**
- Customer interviews focus exclusively on pain points and jobs-to-be-done
- Team believes it understands customer needs but cannot explain which features are truly differentiated

**Fix.** Supplement problem research by asking customers what they would use if your product didn't exist, surfacing the true competitive comparison set. _source: ch07 database example_

---

### **Exhaustive Alternatives List**

Creating an undifferentiated list of every conceivable competitive alternative rather than filtering and ranking by what best-fit customers would realistically use.

**Why harmful.** You spend equal effort worrying about rare edge-case alternatives and lose focus on the real competitive context that matters.

**Signals.**
- List includes dozens of competitors including obscure ones with minimal market presence
- Team spends equal effort worrying about rare edge-case alternatives

**Fix.** Filter the list to only what best-fit customers would see as an alternative, then rank from most to least common, and group clusters. _source: ch07 §ranking-alternatives_

---

## Category 5: Unique Attributes Mistakes

These errors occur in Step 5, where the goal is to isolate what is genuinely different about your product relative to its competitive alternatives.

### **Listing Benefits as Features**

Teams commonly list claimed benefits — such as 'outstanding customer service' or 'very easy to use' — as if they were provable features.

**Why harmful.** These are values or outcomes, not objective attributes. They are unverifiable and indistinguishable from every competitor's claims.

**Signals.**
- Attribute is a superlative claim ('best', 'easiest', 'most responsive')
- No one on the team can describe how to prove the claim
- Every competitor could make the same claim

**Fix.** Replace vague benefit claims with specific, objective, and provable attributes. For example, instead of 'great customer service,' specify 'more support staff than competitors' or 'support team holds industry certifications.' _source: ch08 §Your opinion of your own strengths is irrelevant without proof_

---

### **Dismissing Internally Contested Attributes**

Teams skip listing attributes that some members view as negatives, losing potentially valuable differentiators before they are properly evaluated.

**Why harmful.** What one person sees as a negative (e.g., requiring on-site installation) another may correctly identify as a differentiating value for a specific customer segment.

**Signals.**
- Team skips an attribute because 'it makes us look bad'
- Only unanimous positives make the list

**Fix.** List all attributes regardless of internal disagreement. Defer judgment to the value-mapping step. _source: ch08 §It's OK to list things here that some folks on the team might think of as negative_

---

### **Premature Prioritization of Attributes**

Teams try to decide which attributes are important and which are not during the listing phase.

**Why harmful.** Results in an incomplete set of differentiators. Obvious 'safe' items dominate while genuinely novel attributes are left off.

**Signals.**
- Team debates importance of an attribute before the list is complete
- Short list with obvious 'safe' items only

**Fix.** Defer all prioritization and grouping to Step 6. Focus Step 5 solely on capturing the broadest possible set of unique attributes. _source: ch08 closing paragraph_

---

## Category 6: Value and Segmentation Mistakes

These errors occur in Steps 6 and 7, where the goal is to translate attributes into value themes and identify who cares most about that value.

### **Presenting Features as Value**

Describing a product capability without explaining the benefit or value it delivers to customers.

**Why harmful.** Leaves customers wondering 'So what?' — the feature fails to connect to any goal they are trying to achieve.

**Signals.**
- Feature descriptions use internal technical jargon
- No mention of customer goals or outcomes
- Benefits described as if they were identical to features

**Fix.** Continue asking 'What does that mean for customers?' at each level until you arrive at a concrete goal the customer is trying to achieve. For example: patented fuzzy logic algorithm → faster response times → respond to customers while on the phone → decreased support response times and increased customer satisfaction. _source: ch09 body, 'patented fuzzy logic algorithm' example_

---

### **Demographic / Firmographic-Only Segmentation**

Defining target customers purely by demographic (age, gender) or firmographic (company size, employee count) traits, which fails to capture whether those customers actually care about the value your product delivers.

**Why harmful.** 'Small businesses' or 'men aged 18–30' are not actionable segments — they include people who have no reason to care about your specific value and exclude people who would love it.

**Signals.**
- Segment described as 'small businesses' or 'businesses with 100 to 2,000 employees'
- Segment described as 'men aged 18–30'
- No connection drawn between segment traits and the product's unique value

**Fix.** Replace or augment demographic/firmographic labels with actionable characteristics — behaviors, technology investments, skills gaps, selling motions, or other traits — that are directly tied to caring about your specific value. _source: ch10 §'A useful segmentation, however, needs to go well beyond demographics or firmographics'_

---

### **Broad-Market Targeting**

Targeting the widest possible market on the assumption that more prospects means more sales.

**Why harmful.** Dilutes messaging and makes it harder to convince any single prospect that your solution is best for them. Longer sales cycles, more discounts, lower win rates.

**Signals.**
- Positioning designed to appeal to 'everyone'
- Sales cycles are long and discounts are frequently requested
- Customers like the product but don't love it

**Fix.** Narrow targeting to best-fit prospects, then widen only when the best-fit segment is demonstrably insufficient to meet business goals. _source: ch10 §'The broader your focus, the more difficult it is to connect with prospects'_

---

## Category 7: Market Frame of Reference Mistakes

These errors occur in Step 8, where the goal is to select the market frame of reference that puts your strengths at the center.

### **Defaulting to the Original Market Category**

Staying in the market category that was baked into the product's original conception, even after the product and market have evolved.

**Why harmful.** Prospects constantly compare you unfavorably to a dominant leader you cannot credibly beat on every dimension they measure.

**Signals.**
- The market category was chosen when the product was first conceived and has never been revisited
- Prospects constantly compare you unfavorably to a dominant leader you cannot credibly beat
- Your differentiators cannot overcome the market leader's advantages in every sales conversation

**Fix.** Deliberately re-evaluate the market frame of reference using abductive reasoning, adjacent market analysis, or cautious customer feedback to find the category that best highlights your current strengths. _source: ch11 opening paragraphs_

---

### **Small Company Competing Head to Head Against an Established Leader**

A small or new company positioning itself in a mature market category to win the entire market, directly taking on a dominant incumbent without a clear, decisive competitive advantage.

**Why harmful.** Every prospect's first question is 'How are you better than [market leader]?' and your product is described as a 'cheaper, crappier' version of the leader. You lack the features, customer list, or brand recognition to win that comparison.

**Signals.**
- First question from every prospect is 'How are you better than [market leader]?'
- Your product is described as a 'cheaper, crappier' version of the leader
- You lack the features, customer list, or brand recognition the leader has

**Fix.** Switch to Big Fish, Small Pond — identify a subsegment with unmet needs where your differentiator matters most and the incumbent cannot easily follow. _source: ch11 §When to use the Head to Head style_

---

### **Subsegmenting Without a Genuine Unmet Need**

Targeting a subsegment that is defined by superficial characteristics rather than a truly distinct and important unmet need.

**Why harmful.** The subsegment provides no structural protection. The market leader can trivially address it, collapsing your differentiation.

**Signals.**
- The subsegment differentiation is cosmetic (e.g., color, superficial branding)
- Customers in the subsegment show no reaction or confusion when you describe the special value
- The market leader could trivially add a feature to cover the need

**Fix.** Validate that the subsegment has a clearly definable, important, and provably unmet need — one that the market leader structurally cannot or will not address — before committing to Big Fish, Small Pond. _source: ch11 §When to use the Big Fish, Small Pond style (Bic for Her example)_

---

### **Premature or Under-Resourced Category Creation**

Attempting to create a new market category without the resources, time, or market conditions needed to sustain the long-term educational effort.

**Why harmful.** You do the expensive work of educating the market, then a well-funded competitor enters to harvest the category you built.

**Signals.**
- Investors and analysts cannot understand what market you are in
- You lack funding for a multi-year market education campaign
- Larger competitors begin entering the space as soon as buyers start to understand the category

**Fix.** Either find an existing category that can accommodate your positioning, or secure deep-pocketed patient investors and commit to a long-term plan that defends your leadership position at every stage of category development. _source: ch11 §When to use the Create a New Game style_

---

## Category 8: Trend Mistakes

These errors occur in Step 9, the optional trend-layering step.

### **Trend Without Market Declaration**

Describing a product using only trend language (e.g., 'sharing economy for pets,' 'Uber for cats') without first declaring a clear market category.

**Why harmful.** The product sounds interesting or cool but customers cannot understand what it actually is. Positioning feels cool but generates confused prospects.

**Signals.**
- Product description leads with a trend or hot company name (e.g., 'the X of Y') without naming the market
- Customers frequently ask 'but what does it actually do?'
- Positioning feels cool but generates confused prospects

**Fix.** Lead with the market category first, then layer on the trend. For example, instead of 'sharing economy for pets,' position as 'a marketplace for pet services' and optionally note how it participates in the sharing economy trend. _source: ch12 §trend-without-market warning_

---

### **Trend Without Product Link**

Aligning with a trend — through a name change or content strategy — without demonstrating a concrete connection between the trend and the company's actual solution.

**Why harmful.** Short-term attention gives way to customer and investor confusion when no substantive link is established. Sales team cannot explain how the trend relates to the product's features.

**Signals.**
- Company adopts trend terminology (e.g., adds 'Blockchain' to its name) without a corresponding product strategy
- Content about the trend generates traffic but does not convert to product interest
- Sales team cannot explain how the trend relates to the product's features

**Fix.** Ensure any trend reference is backed by concrete product capabilities, partnerships, or use cases that demonstrate how the trend applies to what you actually sell. _source: ch12 §long-blockchain-example_

## Category 9: Ongoing Mistakes

These errors occur after positioning is set — they prevent positioning from staying current.

### **Messaging Without a Master Document**

Building campaigns and materials without a single canonical messaging document, causing each new piece to build on slightly modified previous messages.

**Why harmful.** Messaging and positioning drift over time. New hires have no agreed-upon language. Different functions describe the product in noticeably different ways.

**Signals.**
- Different sales decks or web pages describe the product in noticeably different ways
- No single source of truth exists for approved product messaging
- New hires can't find agreed-upon language to describe the product

**Fix.** Create and maintain a master messaging document as the accepted baseline; require all new campaigns and copy to start from it. _source: ch14 §Messaging_

---

### **Set-and-Forget Positioning**

Treating positioning as a one-time exercise and never revisiting it, even as competitors enter the market, regulations change, technology shifts, or customer attitudes evolve.

**Why harmful.** Positioning that was accurate at launch becomes stale. Sales team notices declining resonance but has no mechanism to trigger a review.

**Signals.**
- Positioning has not been reviewed in more than six months
- A major competitor has entered the market without any positioning review being triggered
- Sales team notices declining resonance of the current pitch with prospects

**Fix.** Schedule positioning reviews every six months and after any major market event; check customers' perceptions to determine whether repositioning is needed. _source: ch14 §Tracking Your Positioning over Time_

---

## When to Use / When NOT to Use This Reference

**Use this reference when:**
- You are diagnosing why existing positioning isn't resonating with prospects or isn't converting
- You are reviewing a completed positioning exercise to catch mistakes before launching
- You are onboarding a new team member who needs to understand what bad positioning looks like
- A positioning review has been triggered by a market event (new competitor, product pivot, market shift)

**Do NOT treat this as a checklist to complete before starting positioning.** Anti-patterns are diagnostic tools, not a pre-flight checklist. Run the [10-step positioning process](../core/positioning-foundations.md) forward; use this catalog when something feels wrong to name what's broken.

## Code Examples

Anti-patterns in this domain manifest in written positioning artifacts rather than executable code. Here are two representative examples of the pattern and its fix.

### Example 1: Trend Without Market Declaration vs. Corrected

```text
# ANTI-PATTERN: Trend Without Market Declaration
"We're the sharing economy for pets."

# CORRECTED: Market first, trend layer optional
"We're a marketplace for pet services — dog walking, boarding, and grooming —
connecting pet owners with vetted local providers. We're part of the broader
sharing economy trend making it easy to access trusted local services on demand."
```

### Example 2: Demographic Segmentation vs. Actionable Segmentation

```text
# ANTI-PATTERN: Firmographic-Only Segmentation
Target: "Businesses with 100 to 2,000 employees"

# CORRECTED: Actionable Characteristics Tied to Value
Target: "Mid-market companies that have recently migrated to Salesforce but
lack a dedicated RevOps team, causing them to lose deal data between handoffs
— the exact problem our automated sync solves."
```

### Example 3: Feature Listed as Value vs. Full Value Chain

```text
# ANTI-PATTERN: Feature as Value
"Patented fuzzy logic algorithm"

# CORRECTED: Features → Benefits → Value chain
Feature:  Patented fuzzy logic algorithm
Benefit:  Faster query response times
Value:    Support agents can respond to customers in real time
          → Decreased support response times
          → Increased customer satisfaction scores
```
