# Sales Story and Messaging

You've finished the 10-step positioning process — congratulations. Now comes the pain most teams hit immediately: nobody knows how to *use* the positioning. The sales team still pitches the product the old way. Marketing writes new copy that drifts from what you agreed on. Six months later, the carefully crafted positioning lives only in a workshop document no one opens.

This reference covers everything that must happen after positioning is complete: translating it into a concrete sales story, anchoring all copy in a master messaging document, aligning pricing and roadmap to your market category, and keeping positioning fresh as markets change.

---

## The Sales Story Arc

Before anyone writes a word of copy, build the sales story. The author's finding from working with dozens of companies: getting the team to agree on a story arc first produces far better alignment on how positioning becomes a pitch than jumping straight to copywriting. _source: ch14 §Translating Your Positioning into a Sales Story_

A **Sales Story** is a structured narrative arc a salesperson uses to pitch a product. It must follow this arc **in order**:

| Step | Stage | What you cover |
|------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Define the problem | The problem the product was designed to solve |
| 2 | Current solutions and shortcomings | How customers currently attempt to solve it and where those solutions fall short |
| 3 | The Perfect World | What a perfect solution would look like, given the problem and the limitations of current solutions |
| 4 | Introduce the product | The product introduced in its market category |
| 5 | Value themes | The 1–4 value themes that make the product uniquely compelling |
| 6 | Objections and proof | Handling objections; citing customer case studies |
| 7 | Next action | The desired next step for the prospect |

_source: ch14 §Translating Your Positioning into a Sales Story_

This arc applies specifically when building a first-sales-call pitch for a product with a complex B2B sales cycle. It is not intended as a general copywriting template.

### The Perfect World Stage

**The Perfect World** is Step 3 of the arc and deserves particular attention. At this stage, the salesperson describes what the features of a perfect solution would look like — drawing directly from what is known about the problem and the explicit gaps in current solutions. This stage sets up Step 4 so that the product's introduction feels like the inevitable answer rather than a vendor pitch.

### Do Not Write Messaging in the Room

Once the full team has agreed on the sales story arc, the marketing team translates it into copy *independently*. Messaging should not be written in real time with a group. _source: ch14 §Messaging_

## The Master Messaging Document

A **Messaging Document** is a master document that records agreed-upon baseline messaging, gives everyone a common starting point for building specific copy, and prevents positioning and language from drifting as messages are built on modified messages in a chain. _source: ch14 §Messaging_

Without a master document, every new campaign, deck, or web page gets built on top of slightly modified previous messages. Each iteration drifts a little further from the agreed positioning until different parts of the company describe the product in noticeably different ways.

### Anti-pattern: Messaging Without a Master Document

**Messaging Without a Master Document** is the failure mode of building campaigns and materials without a single canonical source, causing each new piece to build on slightly modified previous messages, which causes messaging and positioning to drift over time.

**Why harmful:** Individual contributors optimize their copy locally (for their audience, their channel, their deadline) without visibility into what the agreed positioning says. Cumulative drift is invisible until a new hire can't find consistent language, or until sales decks and the website tell materially different stories.

**Fix:** Create and maintain a master messaging document as the accepted baseline; require all new campaigns and copy to start from it.

**Signals to watch for:**
- Different sales decks or web pages describe the product in noticeably different ways
- No single source of truth exists for approved product messaging
- New hires can't find agreed-upon language to describe the product

## Positioning as Business Strategy: Roadmap and Pricing

Treat a shift in positioning as a shift in business strategy, not merely a marketing exercise. Positioning eventually impacts every department — product roadmap, pricing, sales, and marketing. _source: ch14 §Product Roadmap and Pricing_

The author's illustration: the roadmap for "accounting software" looks really different from the roadmap for "financial services." Positioning shapes what gets built next.

### Align Pricing to the Market Category

After any positioning change that moves the product into a new or redefined market category, pricing must be adjusted to align with that category's price expectations. Pricing reinforces whether the product "belongs" in the category. A premium price signals one kind of market; a per-seat SaaS price signals another. If the price contradicts the category, customers feel cognitive dissonance before the first demo. _source: ch14 §Product Roadmap and Pricing_

## Tracking Positioning Over Time

### Anti-pattern: Set-and-Forget Positioning

**Set-and-Forget Positioning** is treating positioning as a one-time exercise and never revisiting it, even as competitors enter the market, regulations change, technology shifts, or customer attitudes evolve.

**Why harmful:** Both products and markets change over time. Positioning that was strong can be weakened by new competitors, regulation, economic shifts, new technology, or shifting customer attitudes. A pitch that resonated 18 months ago may silently stop working — and you won't know until pipeline starts drying up.

**Fix:** Schedule positioning reviews every six months and after any major market event; check customers' perceptions to determine whether repositioning is needed.

**Signals to watch for:**
- Positioning has not been reviewed in more than six months
- A major competitor has entered the market without any positioning review being triggered
- Sales team notices declining resonance of the current pitch with prospects

### When to Trigger a Positioning Review

Review positioning when **any** of the following occurs:

- Six months have passed since the last review
- A credible, established competitor has entered the market
- A competitor has been acquired and gained significant new resources or reach
- Government regulations have shifted customer priorities
- A significant economic climate change has occurred
- New technology has changed what is possible in the market
- Customer attitudes or buying patterns have noticeably shifted

_source: ch14 §Tracking Your Positioning over Time_

### Should You Reposition When a Large Competitor Enters?

Not automatically. Whether to change positioning in response to a competitive threat should be driven by **customers' perceptions**, not merely by the presence of a new competitor. _source: ch14 §Tracking Your Positioning over Time_

**Reposition when:**
- Target customers expect the new competitor to be relevant to them
- The new competitor's arrival signals a change in customer expectations about price, features, or market norms
- The competitive alternatives list in customers' minds has materially shifted

**Do not reposition when:**
- Target customers do not expect the new competitor to be relevant to them
- Your strategy was to target a subsegment the larger competitor is unlikely to serve better
- Customers do not expect a change in market norms (price, features, etc.) due to the new entrant

### Where to Restart After a Landscape Change

A **Competitive Landscape Change** — a credible established competitor entering the market, an acquisition, new regulation, economic climate shift, or new technology — can weaken or invalidate existing positioning. When such an event signals a shift in how customers think about the market, restart the positioning review at **Step 4 (competitive alternatives)** and work forward from there. _source: ch14 §Tracking Your Positioning over Time_

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use this guidance when:**
- You have completed the 10-step positioning process and need to activate it across the company
- You are building or rebuilding a first-sales-call pitch for a B2B product
- Your company lacks a single canonical messaging source and copy is visibly drifting
- A market event has occurred and you need a framework to decide whether to reposition

**Do NOT use this guidance when:**
- You have not yet completed the positioning process — come back here after finishing Step 10 (positioning over time / full canvas)
- You are writing consumer-facing copy for a low-consideration purchase (the 7-step sales story arc assumes a complex B2B sales cycle)
- You are trying to select or change your market frame of reference — that decision belongs earlier in the process (see [market positioning styles](../patterns/market-positioning-styles.md))

## Code Examples

The sales story arc is the primary structured artifact from this chapter. Use this checklist when preparing a first sales call:

```markdown
## Sales Story Checklist

### Step 1 — Define the Problem
- [ ] Named the specific problem the product was designed to solve
- [ ] Stated why this problem is painful for best-fit customers

### Step 2 — Current Solutions and Shortcomings
- [ ] Listed the top 2–3 competitive alternatives best-fit customers use today
- [ ] Named the specific shortcomings of each alternative

### Step 3 — The Perfect World
- [ ] Described what a perfect solution would look like
- [ ] Tied "perfect world" features directly to the gaps named in Step 2

### Step 4 — Introduce the Product in Its Market Category
- [ ] Named the market category explicitly
- [ ] Stated clearly what the product IS before listing what it does

### Step 5 — Value Themes
- [ ] Presented 1–4 value themes (not a feature list)
- [ ] Each value theme is supported by at least one proof point

### Step 6 — Objections and Case Studies
- [ ] Identified the 2–3 most common objections from best-fit customers
- [ ] Prepared a customer case study for each major objection

### Step 7 — Next Action
- [ ] Defined the single desired next step for this call
- [ ] Made it easy for the prospect to say yes to that step
```

```markdown
## Positioning Review Trigger Log

| 2024-03-02 | Competitor X acquired by enterprise vendor | Yes — 8 customer interviews | Partial     |
| 2024-07-15 | 6-month scheduled review                   | Pending                    | TBD         |
```
