# Value Themes (Step 6): Mapping Attributes to Customer Value

## Problem Framing

You've completed Step 5 and have a list of unique attributes — the specific features and capabilities that set your product apart from competitive alternatives. Now you hit a familiar wall: your team knows exactly what the product *does*, but customers keep responding with "So what?"

The pain is real. You describe a "patented fuzzy logic algorithm" and watch your prospect's eyes glaze over. Your sales deck leads with feature bullets that mean everything to your engineers and nothing to the buyer. You're leaving customers to do the translation work themselves — and most won't bother.

Step 6 solves this. It moves your positioning from the product team's internal taxonomy to the customer's world: the goals they're trying to achieve, the problems they need to solve. The output is one to four **value themes** — coherent clusters of related value points that make your unique attributes instantly relevant to best-fit customers.

---

## The Features → Benefits → Value Chain

The core mechanism of Step 6 is a three-level translation:

| Level | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Feature** | Something the product does or has | Patented fuzzy logic algorithm |
| **Benefit** | What the feature enables for customers | Faster response times |
| **Value** | How it maps to a goal the customer is trying to achieve | Decreased support response times and increased customer satisfaction |

Each level moves closer to the customer's perspective. Features live inside your product. Value lives inside your customer's world.

_source: ch09 Table 1 and surrounding explanation_

### Why you can't skip the translation

> "Unlike consumer tech (e.g., megapixels), most products lack the trained customer vocabulary to make that translation automatic; leaving it implicit risks a 'So what?' response."

For most B2B and complex products, customers have not been trained to automatically convert your feature language into their goal language. Marketers should not assume customers will do this translation on their own. If you describe a feature and stop there, you've handed the prospect a puzzle they didn't ask for.

The fix is to keep asking: **"What does that mean for customers?"** at each level until you land on a concrete goal.

_source: ch09 body, "For most of our products…" paragraph_

### One feature, multiple value points

Each feature can have multiple value points. A combination of features can also provide value together that no single feature provides alone. When you build your features-to-value mapping table, account for this many-to-many relationship — don't assume one feature maps to exactly one value point.

_source: ch09 opening paragraphs_

## Clustering into Value Themes

Once you have the full list of value points, the next step is to group related points into clusters — your **value themes**.

> "Value themes are groupings of related value points — organized from the customer's perspective — that distill a product's unique attributes into one to four coherent clusters of value."

Two rules govern this clustering:

1. **Cluster from the customer's perspective, not the product team's.** What matters is which value points would naturally be related in the minds of customers and prospects — not how your engineering team organized the codebase or how your product roadmap is structured.

2. **Aim for one to four clusters.** Reduce the full list of value points down to one to four value themes. It is not uncommon for this exercise to produce just a single value theme when your product has a tightly focused differentiation. More than four clusters typically means you haven't finished the grouping work.

_source: ch09 §Clustering the Value into 'Themes'_

### Why one to four?

Positioning should bring **unique value to the front and center**, not highlight every feature and attribute that customers love. The goal is to make the most compelling case for best-fit customers — and a long list of disconnected value points dilutes rather than strengthens that case. Customers remember themes, not feature inventories.

---

## Anti-Pattern: Presenting Features as Value

**Presenting Features as Value** is the most common failure mode at this step.

**What it looks like:**
- Product descriptions that use internal technical jargon with no bridge to outcomes
- Benefits described as if they were identical to the underlying features ("Our platform uses real-time indexing" instead of "you find answers in seconds instead of minutes")
- No mention of customer goals or outcomes anywhere in the positioning copy

**Why it's harmful:** It forces prospects to do translation work they're not equipped to do. A "patented fuzzy logic algorithm" is meaningless to a customer who just wants shorter support queues. When customers can't translate the feature into their own goal language, they disengage — or worse, they accept a competitor's simpler story instead.

**The fix:** At every level of the features → benefits → value chain, keep asking "What does that mean for customers?" Don't stop until you've arrived at a concrete goal the customer is trying to achieve.

_source: ch09 body, "patented fuzzy logic algorithm" example_

---

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use Step 6 when:**
- You have completed Step 5 and have a confirmed list of unique attributes
- You are preparing positioning copy, a sales narrative, or a messaging document
- Your current messaging leads with feature descriptions and you're getting "So what?" responses from prospects

**Do NOT use Step 6 to:**
- Pad thin differentiation — if your unique attributes are weak, value-theming them won't fix the underlying problem (go back to Steps 4 and 5)
- Invent customer goals that your attributes don't actually support — value themes must be traceable back to real unique attributes
- Create more than four themes just to look comprehensive — more clusters means less clarity, not more

## Code Examples

The following table illustrates the features → benefits → value chain in practice, following the book's structure:

```
Feature (what the product has/does)
  └─► Benefit (what it enables for customers)
        └─► Value (goal the customer is trying to achieve)

Example:
  Feature:  Patented fuzzy logic algorithm
  Benefit:  Faster response times
  Value:    Decreased support response times and increased customer satisfaction

Example (multi-value):
  Feature:  Real-time call transcription
  Benefit:  Agents can review what was said without replaying audio
  Value 1:  Faster post-call documentation → reduced handle time
  Value 2:  Accurate records → fewer escalation errors

Clustering:
  Value points {faster handle time, fewer escalations, reduced callbacks}
    → Value Theme: "Support Efficiency"

  Value points {agent confidence, consistent responses, onboarding speed}
    → Value Theme: "Team Readiness"
```

The full exercise produces one to four of these clusters. Each cluster becomes a value theme in your positioning and, eventually, a pillar in your sales story.
