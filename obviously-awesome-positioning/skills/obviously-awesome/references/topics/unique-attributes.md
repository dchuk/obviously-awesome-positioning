# Unique Attributes (Step 5)

You've mapped your competitive alternatives and you're ready to explain what makes your product different. The trap most teams fall into here: they reach for benefit language — "outstanding customer service," "easy to use," "best-in-class support" — instead of the underlying, provable capabilities that actually differentiate the product. Step 5 exists to force that distinction.

The pain is real. If you let benefit claims stand in for attributes, your positioning ends up sounding identical to every competitor's. Every SaaS vendor claims easy onboarding. Every B2B vendor claims great support. When you can't prove it, you can't own it.

Step 5's job is narrow: **list every attribute your product or company has or does that competitive alternatives do not.** Don't rank them. Don't group them. Don't decide what matters yet — that's Step 6. Just capture the full, honest set of differentiators so the value-mapping work in [Step 6](value-themes.md) has real material to work with.

---

## What "Unique Attribute" Means

An attribute is a capability or characteristic that a product or company **has or does** — distinct from the value it drives for customers. The distinction is load-bearing. "Fast query execution" is an attribute. "Saves analysts time" is the value it drives. You need the attribute first; the value mapping comes later.

Attributes can live at the product level or the company level:

- **Product-level:** specific features, technical capabilities, integrations, performance benchmarks
- **Company-level:** proprietary processes, domain expertise, distribution channels, partnerships, certifications, team composition

_source: ch08 §Be broad and creative_

Cast a wide net. Differentiation can come from sources your team might not think of as "features." A service firm's methodology, a vendor's exclusive data set, a startup's founding team's patents — all of these are fair game.

---

## Provability Is Non-Negotiable

> "Your opinion of your own strengths is irrelevant without proof." _source: ch08 §Your opinion of your own strengths is irrelevant without proof_

A claimed attribute that cannot be objectively verified does not hold up in the market. The test: **can you prove this claim with objective, verifiable facts?**

Proof sources that count:
- Directly measurable facts (benchmark numbers, feature counts, certifications)
- Third-party validation: an independent reviewer, analyst report, or approved customer quote that confirms the attribute is better than the alternative

Proof sources that do NOT count:
- Internal team opinion ("we think our support is great")
- Superlative language with no backing ("easiest to use," "most responsive")
- Claims every competitor could make identically

If your team can't describe *how* you'd prove a claim, that claim is not a unique attribute — it's a wish. Either find the proof or rephrase it as a specific, measurable capability.

---

## Consideration Attributes vs. Retention Attributes

Not all genuine differentiators belong in your core positioning. Step 5 asks you to distinguish between two types:

**Consideration attributes** — features customers care about when evaluating whether to buy. These are directly connected to a goal the customer wants to accomplish *right now*, at purchase time. These belong in positioning.

**Retention attributes** — features that matter at renewal time: ease of doing business, ongoing support quality, account management responsiveness. These are real and valuable, but they don't influence whether a customer puts you on their evaluation list.

> "Customers who don't see enough value to consider buying the product will never stick around long enough to experience retention attributes such as excellent customer service." _source: ch08 §Concentrate on consideration rather than retention attributes_

The practical rule: if the attribute only matters *after* the customer has already purchased, deprioritize it in positioning and surface it in renewal or expansion messaging instead.

---

## The Listing Process

The rule is simple: **list everything, prioritize nothing.**

Include:
- Attributes your whole team agrees are differentiating
- Attributes some team members see as negative or embarrassing (e.g., requiring on-site installation, narrower feature scope) — what looks like a weakness in one segment may be a genuine value signal in another
- Attributes whose value to customers is uncertain

Exclude nothing at this stage. Grouping and prioritization happen in Step 6. If you start debating importance during Step 5, you'll prematurely kill differentiators that haven't had a chance to prove their value through the value-mapping exercise.

---

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use Step 5 guidance when:**
- You are working through the 10-step positioning process and have completed Step 4 (competitive alternatives)
- You are repositioning an existing product and need to audit which claims are real vs. assumed
- Your team is writing positioning copy and realizes the benefit language has no provable foundation

**Do NOT apply this guidance when:**
- You haven't completed [Step 4 (competitive alternatives)](competitive-alternatives.md) — unique attributes are only meaningful relative to specific alternatives
- You are doing brand messaging work that intentionally operates at the value/outcome level; in that context, benefit language is appropriate and expected
- You are in Step 6 or later — by then, you've already captured the attribute list and should be mapping values, not still listing features

## Code Examples

The following checklist and decision table operationalize the step.

### Attribute inclusion decision

```
Should this candidate item be included in the unique attributes list?

YES when:
  ✓ The product or company has or does this — competitive alternatives do not
  ✓ The claim is based on objective facts and is provable
  ✓ Third-party validation (analyst, reviewer, customer quote) supports the claim
  ✓ The attribute connects to a goal the customer wants to accomplish at purchase time

NO when:
  ✗ The claim is a benefit or value statement, not a feature (e.g., "outstanding
    customer service" without supporting facts)
  ✗ No one on the team can describe how to prove the claim
  ✗ Every competitor could (and does) make the same claim
  ✗ The attribute only matters post-purchase (retention, not consideration)
```
_source: ch08 §Your opinion of your own strengths is irrelevant without proof_

## Anti-Patterns

**Listing Benefits as Features** — Teams write "outstanding customer service" or "very easy to use" and treat these as attributes. They aren't. They're outcomes. Every competitor claims them, none can prove them, and positioning built on them is invisible. Fix: replace every superlative claim with a specific, measurable capability backed by data or third-party validation. _source: ch08 §Your opinion of your own strengths is irrelevant without proof_

**Dismissing Internally Contested Attributes** — Teams skip attributes that some members view as negatives, losing potentially valuable differentiators before they're properly evaluated. What looks like a weakness (requiring on-site installation, narrower feature scope) may be a strong signal for a specific customer segment. Fix: list all attributes regardless of internal disagreement; evaluation happens in Step 6. _source: ch08 §It's OK to list things here that some folks on the team might think of as negative_

**Premature Prioritization of Attributes** — Teams debate importance during the listing phase and produce a short, "safe" list. This starves the value-mapping step of raw material. Fix: defer all prioritization and grouping to Step 6; Step 5's only job is completeness. _source: ch08 closing paragraph_
