# Competitive Alternatives (Step 4)

## Problem Framing

You know your product's features. You know the problems your customers face. But when you sit down to write positioning, you find yourself listing direct feature competitors — the products that look most like yours from the inside. The result: positioning that resonates with your engineering team but confuses your prospects.

The pain is specific: customers keep comparing you to things you didn't expect. A prospect says "we're just using Excel." Another says "we hired an intern for that." Neither is on your competitor list. Your win/loss analysis makes no sense because you're not even competing in the category your customers think you're in.

The fix is Step 4 of the positioning process: listing your **true competitive alternatives** — the real alternatives best-fit customers would use if your product did not exist, defined entirely from the customer's perspective.

> "Product features and the value they provide are only unique, interesting, and valuable when a customer perceives them in relation to alternatives." _source: ch07 opening paragraphs_

You cannot know which of your features are differentiating — or how to frame your strengths — until you know what customers are actually comparing you to.

---

## The Core Question

Step 4 has a single driving question:

> **"What would our best customers do if we didn't exist?"**

This is not a hypothetical. Ask it of real best-fit customers in interviews. Ask it in win/loss calls. Ask it in onboarding surveys. Their answers — not your product roadmap, not your feature matrix — build the list of true competitive alternatives. _source: ch07 §what-would-customers-do_

Notice what the question is *not*: it does not ask customers to describe their problem. Customers are often poor at articulating problems with the nuance that reveals competitive categories. Problem-focused answers do not surface the actual comparison set. You need both — but problem research alone leaves you flying blind on positioning. _source: ch07 database example_

---

## Customer-Perceived Competition vs. Product-Centric Views

There are two fundamentally different ways to build a competitor list:

**Product-centric view (wrong for positioning):** Start with your product and its features. Look for products that do similar things. List them. This reflects the product creator's expert knowledge of the solution space — knowledge that typical customers do not have.

**Customer-perceived competition (right for positioning):** Ask best-fit customers what they would actually use as a replacement. Use their answers. This reveals the category customers mentally place your product in and the strengths and weaknesses they will compare you on.

Customers' perception of competitors is the only one that matters for positioning. _source: ch07 opening paragraphs_

When these two views diverge — and they often diverge dramatically — the product-centric view produces positioning that feels internally logical but externally weak.

### Signals you have a product-centric competitor view

- Your competitor list consists only of products that look like direct feature competitors.
- Your team is surprised when customers name non-obvious alternatives.
- Positioning fails to resonate with prospects.

## Building the List

### Step 1: Ask best-fit customers

Focus on your best-fit customers — the customers for whom your product delivers the most value and who are most representative of your general target market. Do not anchor on edge-case customers with unusual competitive awareness; their obscure alternatives have little mindshare among your prospects and will dilute your positioning effort. _source: ch07 §ranking-alternatives_

### Step 2: Rank from most to least common

Once you have a raw list of alternatives from customer interviews, rank them by frequency. How often does each alternative appear when you ask best-fit customers "what would you do without us?" Focus on the most common answers. _source: ch07 §ranking-alternatives_

### Step 3: Filter out low-mindshare options

Do not list a competitor as a true competitive alternative if they have a small number of customers and therefore likely zero mindshare with prospects. A competitor your team knows well but that your customers have never heard of is not a true competitive alternative for positioning purposes. _source: ch07 §ranking-alternatives_

### Step 4: Group into clusters

After filtering and ranking, group similar alternatives together. For example, "do it manually" might cover "hire an intern," "do it myself with Excel," and "ask the admin team." This grouping simplifies analysis and sets you up cleanly for Step 5 (unique attributes) and Step 6 (value themes).

Expect and aim for **a minimum of two and a maximum of five groups**. _source: ch07 closing paragraphs_

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use this step when:**
- You are beginning a positioning exercise (this is Step 4 in the ten-step process).
- You are repositioning a product and need to reset your competitive frame.
- Win/loss analysis is producing results that don't make sense — often a signal that your assumed competitive set is wrong.
- Sales is struggling to explain how you're different, which usually means they're comparing against the wrong alternatives.

**Do not skip or shortcut this step when:**
- You think you already know your competitors. Experts in their own product category are the most likely to have a product-centric view.
- Your product is new or has no obvious direct competitors. The absence of feature-alike competitors does not mean customers have no alternatives — they do ("do nothing," "hire someone," "build it in-house").

**This step is less applicable when:**
- You are doing messaging or copy work without a full positioning review — though you should still be anchored to a previously completed competitive alternatives list.

## Code Examples

The following decision rules from the book can be applied directly in a positioning workshop:

```
Decision: Should a potential competitor be included in the true competitive alternatives list?

YES when:
  - A meaningful number of best-fit customers would actually use this alternative
    if your product didn't exist
  - The alternative has mindshare among your target prospects
  - The alternative appears frequently when asking best-fit customers
    "what would you do without us?"

NO when:
  - The competitor has very few customers and therefore likely zero mindshare
    with prospects
  - Only edge-case or atypical customers would consider this alternative
  - The alternative is only visible to product creators with deep expert knowledge
    of the solution space, not to typical customers
```

_source: ch07 §ranking-alternatives_

```
Decision: Should competitive alternatives be grouped into clusters?

YES when:
  - Alternatives naturally cluster into similar categories
    (e.g., multiple "do it manually" approaches)
  - Grouping helps the team move to the next positioning step

NO when:
  - Alternatives are so distinct that no natural grouping exists

Target: minimum 2 groups, maximum 5 groups
```

_source: ch07 closing paragraphs_

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Product-Centric Competitor View** — Starting from the product and its features to determine competitors, rather than asking what customers would actually use as a replacement. This produces a competitor list that reflects product creator expertise, not customer mental models. Fix: ask best-fit customers what they would do if your product didn't exist. _source: ch07 opening paragraphs_

**Problem-Only Customer Research** — Asking customers only about the problems they are trying to solve, without asking about alternatives. Problem-focused answers do not reveal the competitive category customers place you in. Fix: supplement problem research with the "what would you do without us?" question. _source: ch07 database example_

**Exhaustive Alternatives List** — Listing every conceivable competitive alternative rather than filtering and ranking by what best-fit customers would realistically use. This spreads positioning focus across too many targets. Fix: filter to best-fit customer alternatives only, rank from most to least common, and group into 2–5 clusters. _source: ch07 §ranking-alternatives_
