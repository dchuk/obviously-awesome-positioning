# Target Segmentation: Determining Who Cares a Lot

You've mapped your unique attributes to value themes — but value is only valuable to someone. Step 7 of the positioning process asks: who, specifically, cares most intensely about that value? Getting this wrong is expensive: broad targeting stretches limited sales and marketing budgets across prospects who will never convert, while narrow targeting focused on best-fit prospects produces faster closes, fuller prices, and word-of-mouth growth.

## The Real Problem with "Segmentation"

Most teams default to demographic or firmographic labels: "small businesses," "companies with 100–2,000 employees," "men aged 18–30." These feel like segmentation, but they aren't actionable. Knowing a prospect employs 500 people tells you nothing about whether they care about what your product does.

> "A useful segmentation, however, needs to go well beyond demographics or firmographics." _source: ch10_

The goal is a list of a prospect's **easily identifiable characteristics that make them really care about what you do** — behaviors, technology investments, skills gaps, selling motions, strategic priorities — anything that maps directly to the value your product delivers.

## Best-Fit Prospects

**Best-fit prospects** are customers whose easily identifiable characteristics make them care a lot about the specific value your product delivers. The book's description of their behavior makes the business case plain: they buy quickly, pay full price, evangelize the product, and rarely churn. _source: ch10_

These customers are your anchor. Step 7 is the exercise of describing them precisely enough that a sales rep can find them in the wild.

## The "Who Cares and Why?" Loop

The mechanical process of Step 7 is iterative. For each value point you identified in Step 6, ask:

**Who cares and why?**

> "You need to focus on the value points that you listed in the previous step and repeat the question, Who cares and why?" _source: ch10_

You are mapping the value your product's features deliver to the group of customers with the highest affinity for your product. Run the loop for every value theme, then look for overlap — the segment that appears most consistently across all value points is your best-fit target.

## Actionable vs. Firmographic Segmentation

| Firmographic-only (weak) | Actionable (strong) |
|---|---|
| "Small businesses" | "Bootstrapped SaaS companies with a solo founder handling both sales and support" |
| "Companies with 100–2,000 employees" | "Mid-market B2B companies that recently migrated to Salesforce but lack a dedicated RevOps function" |
| "Men aged 18–30" | "First-time homebuyers who completed an online mortgage pre-qualification in the last 90 days" |

The right column describes characteristics that are (a) identifiable before a sale and (b) directly tied to caring about your specific value.

## The Narrow-to-Win Principle

> "Target as narrowly as you can to meet your near-term sales objectives." _source: ch10_

Broader focus makes it **harder**, not easier, to connect with and convert prospects. When messaging must appeal to everyone, it resonates with no one. Best-fit customers buy faster, require less discounting, and advocate for your product. Diluting focus to a broad audience wastes limited sales and marketing resources.

This is counterintuitive. Teams often assume that reaching more people produces more sales. In practice, **Broad-Market Targeting** — targeting the widest possible market on the assumption that more prospects means more sales — dilutes messaging and makes it harder to convince any single prospect that your solution is best for them. _source: ch10_

**Signals you've fallen into this anti-pattern:**
- Positioning designed to appeal to "everyone"
- Long sales cycles with frequent discount requests
- Customers like the product but don't love it

## Two Criteria for a Worthwhile Segment

Not every narrow segment is the right segment. A candidate segment must meet at least two criteria to be worth focusing on:

1. **Large enough** — it must be big enough to meet the goals of your business
2. **Specifically underserved** — it must have important, specific, unmet needs that are common across the segment _source: ch10_

If a segment fails either test, move on. A tiny segment with sharp unmet needs can't generate sufficient revenue. A large segment without a shared unmet need can't be addressed with a single positioning.

## When the Math Doesn't Work: Broadening

Narrow targeting is the default, but it has a limit. If your best-fit segment alone is too small to hit your near-term sales targets, broaden your definition of "best-fit" until the numbers work. _source: ch10_

The question to ask explicitly:

> "Could you hit your targets by focusing on only your best-fit customers?" _source: ch10_

If yes, stay narrow. If no, expand the definition of best-fit — not by reverting to vague demographics, but by identifying additional characteristics that correlate with caring about your value — until the segment is large enough.

## Positioning Evolves

Your target segment description is not permanent. Positioning — including who you target — should reflect your best-fit customers **right now** and evolve over time.

> "Keep in mind that your product positioning will constantly be evolving." _source: ch10_

Attempting to future-proof positioning prevents you from being sharply relevant to today's best-fit customers. Build positioning that wins with the segment you can reach today, then revisit as your product capabilities, competitive landscape, and customer base change.

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use this step when:**
- You have completed Step 6 (value themes) and need to translate value into a target audience
- Your current messaging is generating indifferent responses or long, painful sales cycles
- You are repositioning and need to reset who you are selling to, not just what you say

**Do NOT use this step to:**
- Pre-define a target segment before working through Steps 4–6 (competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value themes) — segmentation must be grounded in the value you actually deliver
- Lock in a permanent, future-proof target audience — treat any segmentation as a current snapshot to be revisited
- Replace firmographics with a single behavioral trait and call it done — the most actionable segments combine multiple identifiable characteristics

## Code Examples

The following decision framework applies the two-criteria rule and the narrow-vs-broaden decision in sequence.

```
Step 7 Segmentation Checklist
─────────────────────────────

1. For each value theme from Step 6, ask: "Who cares and why?"
   → List the characteristics that make a prospect care about this value.

2. Look for overlap across all value themes.
   → The characteristics that appear repeatedly define your best-fit profile.

3. Apply the two-criteria test to the candidate segment:
   [ ] Is the segment large enough to meet near-term sales goals?
   [ ] Does the segment have important, specific, unmet needs common to all members?

   If both → proceed with this segment.
   If either fails → either discard and try a different segment,
                     OR broaden the definition of best-fit.

4. Apply the narrow-vs-broaden decision:
   Could you hit your sales targets by selling ONLY to this segment?
   → Yes: lock in the narrow definition.
   → No:  expand 'best-fit' characteristics until the math works,
          then re-apply the two-criteria test.

5. Document the segment as a list of IDENTIFIABLE characteristics
   (behaviors, technology stack, skills gaps, strategic priorities)
   NOT as firmographic labels alone.

6. Mark the segmentation as current-state only.
   Schedule a review trigger (e.g., after 100 new customers,
   or when a major competitive change occurs).
```

```
Example: Weak vs. Strong Segmentation

WEAK (firmographic-only):
  Target: "Mid-market B2B companies, 200–1,000 employees"

STRONG (actionable):
  Target: "Series B–C B2B SaaS companies that:
    - recently hired their first VP of Sales
    - are running a quota-carrying outbound team for the first time
    - do not yet have a dedicated sales operations function
    - are experiencing pipeline visibility problems (board asks for
      forecast accuracy they cannot currently provide)"

The strong version ties each characteristic directly to the
value theme: 'accurate, low-overhead sales forecasting.'
```
