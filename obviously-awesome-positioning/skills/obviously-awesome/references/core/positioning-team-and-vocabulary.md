# Positioning Team and Vocabulary

You can't position a product well in a silo. When one function — typically marketing — owns positioning in isolation, the rest of the organization fails to adopt it. Sales runs their own narrative. Product builds for a different customer. Customer success onboards against a different value story. The result is internal confusion that customers eventually sense.

The fix is deliberate: assemble a cross-functional team, establish shared vocabulary before you begin, and consciously surface the historical assumptions — the **positioning baggage** — that will otherwise silently constrain every decision you make.

---

## Step 2: Form a Positioning Team

### Who must be in the room

Positioning is a business strategy exercise. It is too broad and too important to live in one silo of the company and impacts every group in the organization. That means the person who owns the business strategy must be in the room and must be seen as driving the effort.

- **Startups:** the CEO or founder
- **Larger companies:** the division or business unit leader

Marketing cannot "own" positioning in the same way marketing cannot "own" the overall business strategy. _source: ch05 §Who should be there?_

### Which functions to include

Include one or at most two senior representatives from each functional group:

| Function | Why they belong |
|---|---|
| **Sales / BD** | They hear objections and competitive comparisons daily |
| **Marketing** | They translate positioning into messaging and campaigns |
| **Customer Success** | They see how customers actually use and value the product post-sale |
| **Product / Engineering** | They know what the product can and cannot do today and on the roadmap |

Teams as small as three and as large as twelve have worked. The constraint is not absolute size — it is representation balance. More than two voices from any single function risks that group dominating discussion and drowning out quieter functions.

You want engagement from every group because you need buy-in from every group. Because positioning impacts all functions in significant ways, leaders of each business function must agree with and execute the positioning, so they should be involved in developing it. _source: ch05 §You want engagement from every group_

### What each function gets out of positioning

Positioning produces downstream outputs for every function — another reason cross-functional representation matters:

- **Marketing:** messaging, audience targeting, campaign development
- **Sales / BD:** target customer segmentation, account strategy
- **Customer Success:** onboarding approach, account expansion strategy
- **Product / Dev:** roadmap prioritization, feature framing

_source: ch05 §Consider these outputs that all flow from positioning_

### The external facilitator recommendation

Bringing in an experienced external facilitator to guide the positioning discussion is highly recommended. An outside facilitator makes the exercise more productive and balanced, ensures every voice is heard, and can gently challenge long-held assumptions that insiders treat as untouchable. _source: ch05 §Who will facilitate the discussion?_

Internal senior employees may unconsciously protect historical decisions. An external facilitator has no such investment.

## Step 3: Align Your Positioning Vocabulary and Let Go of Positioning Baggage

### Why vocabulary alignment comes first

Before your team attempts to develop new positioning together, everyone must share an understanding of what positioning actually means. Without it, participants talk past each other — one person's "market" is another's "segment," one person's "feature" is another's "value."

Spend the first hour of any positioning workshop aligning the team on:

1. What positioning means and why it is important
2. Which components make up a position and how each is defined
3. How market maturity and competitive landscape impact positioning style

_source: ch06 opening paragraphs_

### Positioning baggage: the invisible constraint

**Positioning baggage** is the accumulated historical assumptions, terminology, and mental models that product creators carry about their product — rooted in its conception, evolution, and launch — which prevent them from seeing new or better ways to position it. Customers encounter the product fresh and have no such baggage.

The problem: your product's history is baked into the words you use, the category you named at launch, and the use cases you've always led with. None of that is visible to a new customer. All of it shapes — and can distort — how your team frames positioning discussions.

Most products can be many things to many types of buyers, and the best position may be very different from the one originally imagined. Markets shift, and a product's strongest attributes may be far more valuable in a different context than the one for which it was originally created. _source: ch06 §'Let go of your positioning baggage'_

### How to surface and suspend baggage

In order to consider possible new ways to think about a product, the team must consciously set aside their old ways of thinking about it. The steps:

1. **Call out where history lives in your current positioning.** Walk through your current market category choice, your feature terminology, and your target customer description. For each one, ask: "Is this here because it's the best framing today, or because it's how we've always described it?"
2. **Name the baggage explicitly.** Labeling it reduces its grip. "We've always called this an 'enterprise document management tool' because that's how we pitched it in 2019 — let's hold that lightly."
3. **Get team agreement to suspend current positioning opinions** for the duration of the exercise. Without explicit agreement, team members will anchor on existing assumptions and resist alternatives, undermining the process.

Being conscious of how history has shaped current positioning enables everyone to be open to alternatives rather than defending the status quo unconsciously. _source: ch06 §'Let go of your positioning baggage'_

## Anti-Patterns

### **Positioning Exercise Without Business Leader**

Running a positioning exercise that is not driven by the business leader — e.g., delegating the effort entirely to marketing — results in a positioning that the broader organization fails to adopt.

**Why harmful:** Positioning is a business strategy exercise. When the business owner is absent, every other function treats the output as a marketing document they can ignore or override. The positioning never takes hold.

**Fix.** Ensure the CEO, founder, or relevant division/business unit leader is present, visible, and driving the positioning effort from the start.

**Signals:**
- Marketing department is solely "owning" positioning
- Business leader is absent from positioning discussions
- Positioning outputs are not being adopted across functions

_source: ch05 §Who should be there?_

---

### **Overstaffed Positioning Meeting**

Having too many people in the positioning meeting makes it hard to hear from each group and risks one or two groups dominating the discussion.

**Why harmful:** When one function floods the room, quieter functions lose their voice. The resulting position reflects one group's perspective — typically marketing or product — rather than the full cross-functional reality.

**Fix.** Limit representation to one or at most two senior people per functional group.

**Signals:**
- More than two representatives from any single function
- Certain groups consistently dominate discussion
- Quieter functions cannot get their perspectives heard

_source: ch05 §Although there is great value in having representation_

---

### **Carrying Positioning Baggage Into New Positioning Work**

Allowing the product's historical framing — its original intended market, early terminology, and founding assumptions — to unconsciously constrain positioning discussions, preventing the team from seeing better alternatives.

**Why harmful:** The team will re-derive the same position they've always had, because every instinct pulls toward familiar language and familiar categories. Genuine repositioning requires a conscious break from that gravity.

**Fix.** Explicitly surface where the product's history appears in current positioning (terminology, market choice, feature descriptions), acknowledge it as baggage, and get team agreement to suspend current positioning opinions for the duration of the exercise.

**Signals:**
- Current market category is the same one imagined when the product was conceived
- Terminology used to describe features hasn't changed since launch
- Founders or long-tenured employees dominate positioning discussions with historical rationale

_source: ch06 §'Let go of your positioning baggage'_

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use this guidance when:**
- You are starting a positioning or repositioning exercise for any product
- You are preparing a team that has never worked through positioning together
- Internal alignment on product narrative has broken down between functions
- Positioning outputs (messaging, sales story, roadmap framing) are inconsistent across teams

**Do NOT skip this guidance when:**
- You think your team already agrees on positioning — surface the vocabulary anyway; apparent agreement often masks different underlying assumptions
- Your team is small (three people) — even then, one-to-two-per-function and vocabulary alignment apply
- You are repositioning a mature product — the baggage problem is actually *worse* for established products with long histories

**This guidance does not cover:**
- The content of positioning itself (what your market frame, competitive alternatives, or value themes should be) — those are covered in the component and topic references below

## Code Examples

The positioning team vocabulary checklist, drawn directly from the book's minimum shared understanding rule:

```
Positioning Vocabulary Alignment Checklist (first hour of workshop)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
□ 1. What is positioning?
      Definition: the act of deliberately defining how you are the best
      at delivering something a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about.

□ 2. Which five (plus one) components make up a position?
      1. Competitive alternatives
      2. Unique attributes
      3. Value (and proof)
      4. Target market characteristics (best-fit customers)
      5. Market frame of reference
      +1. Trends (optional)

□ 3. How does market maturity affect positioning style?
      - Head to Head: compete in an existing, well-understood category
      - Big Fish Small Pond: own a subsegment of an existing category
      - Create a New Game: define an entirely new category

□ 4. What is positioning baggage?
      Historical assumptions about market, terminology, and use case —
      baked in at conception and launch — that constrain how the team
      sees the product today.

□ 5. Team agreement: we will suspend our current positioning opinions
      for the duration of this exercise.
```

Positioning baggage audit prompt (run before Step 4):

```
Positioning Baggage Audit
─────────────────────────
For each item below, answer: "Is this here because it's the best
framing today, or because it's how we've always described it?"

Market category:    [current category name]
  → Why this category? When was this decision made?

Feature terminology: [list key terms used in sales/marketing]
  → Are these customer words or internal words?

Target customer:    [current description]
  → Based on current best-fit customers, or original intended buyers?

Primary use case:   [current lead use case]
  → Is this the use case we win most often, or the one we built for?

Record any "we've always done it this way" answers as explicit baggage
to set aside before beginning Steps 4–9.
```
