# The Five (Plus One) Components of Effective Positioning

## Problem Framing

You need to position your product. So you open a marketing textbook, find the traditional positioning statement template, and fill it in:

> FOR [target buyers], [product] IS A [market category] WHICH PROVIDES [benefits] UNLIKE [primary competitor] WHICH PROVIDES [competitor's benefits].

You file it away. Six months later, nobody on your team can remember what it said. Sales still pitches the product the same way they always have. Marketing still defaults to the same category framing as the market leader. Nothing changed.

This is the **Using the Traditional Positioning Statement** anti-pattern. The template assumes you already know the best answers. It reinforces status-quo thinking. It produces output that is hard to remember and that teams never actually use. It gives no guidance on whether positioning is good or what to do next.

The fix is to replace the positioning statement exercise with five (plus one) concrete, ordered components that guide you through creative discovery rather than status-quo confirmation.

---

## The Five (Plus One) Components

Positioning breaks down into five required components plus one optional bonus. Each component has a relationship to the others — you must work through them in order, because the output of each step feeds the next.

### 1. Competitive Alternatives

**What target customers would use or do if your product didn't exist.**

This is not limited to direct product competitors. It includes spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring an intern, or doing nothing. Uniqueness is always *relative* — an attribute is only unique when compared against real competitive alternatives from the customer's perspective.

> Being 'easier to use' than competing startups is irrelevant if customers compare your product to Excel; uniqueness must be defined against the actual yardstick customers use. _source: ch03 §2. Unique attributes_

### 2. Unique Attributes

**The features and capabilities your offering has that the competitive alternatives lack.**

Unique attributes can include technical features, delivery model, business model, or specific expertise. A claimed differentiator that is not actually unique versus the real alternatives customers consider is not a differentiator at all.

### 3. Value (and Proof)

**The benefit that unique attributes enable for customers.**

Value must be fact-based and provable in an objective and demonstrable way. Customer, reviewer, or expert opinions and data constitute proof. The company's own opinion does not count.

> Qualitative value claims are too subjective and customers won't believe them; data and expert opinion are difficult to refute. _source: ch03 §3. Value (and proof)_

### 4. Target Market Characteristics

**The specific characteristics of a group of buyers that lead them to care the most about the value you deliver.**

These are the customers who buy quickly, rarely ask for discounts, and tell their friends about your offering. Target market characteristics must go beyond broad firmographic labels (industry, location, company size) to identify the specific characteristic that makes a customer love the product more than others.

### 5. Market Category

**A frame of reference for target customers that helps them understand your product's unique value.**

Declaring a market category triggers a powerful set of assumptions in customers' minds about competitors, expected functionality, and pricing. A well-chosen category leverages those assumptions to work for you. A poor category — the **Choosing the Wrong Market Category** anti-pattern — forces you to spend every sales and marketing interaction battling incorrect assumptions instead of selling value.

Signals that you are in the wrong category:
- Prospects consistently ask for features the product does not have
- Customers love the product but prospects can't figure it out
- Key unique features are not expected or valued in the declared market category

### 6. (Bonus) Relevant Trends

**Trends that target customers understand and are interested in, used in combination with a market category to make a product more relevant right now.**

Trends differ from market categories: a market category is a collection of similar products; a trend is a new and noteworthy characteristic at a given point in time. Using a trend in positioning is optional but often desirable — trends supplement market category context but should never replace it.

> Trends alone (e.g., 'AI') tell customers little about what a product is; the market category must anchor understanding first. _source: ch03 §6. (Bonus) Relevant trends_

## The Ordered Sequence

Always define the components in this exact order:

| Step | Component | Why it comes here |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | Competitive alternatives | Sets the yardstick for everything else |
| 2 | Unique attributes | Only meaningful relative to alternatives |
| 3 | Value and proof | Flows from what unique attributes enable |
| 4 | Target market characteristics | Who cares most about that specific value |
| 5 | Market category | Frame that puts your strengths in best context |
| 6 | Relevant trends (optional) | Makes the category feel timely and urgent |

Starting elsewhere produces positioning that doesn't connect with how customers actually evaluate a solution. The **Defining Features Before Competitive Alternatives** anti-pattern — starting the positioning process by listing key features or value without first establishing competitive alternatives — is one of the most common mistakes teams make.

---

## Why the Positioning Statement Fails

The traditional FOR/IS A/WHICH PROVIDES/UNLIKE template fails for four reasons:

1. **It assumes you already know the answers.** The template gives no process for discovering whether your current framing is optimal.
2. **It reinforces status-quo thinking.** The **Status Quo / Default Positioning** anti-pattern keeps companies blind to alternative framings that might serve them better.
3. **It produces forgettable output.** Teams complete the exercise and file it away, never referring back to it.
4. **It offers no quality signal.** After filling in the blanks, you have no way to evaluate whether the result is good positioning or weak positioning.

The five (plus one) components framework replaces the template with a guided discovery process. Working through each component in sequence surfaces alternatives you would never have considered by filling in a blank.

---

## When to Use / When NOT to Use

**Use this framework when:**
- You are positioning a product for the first time
- You are repositioning an existing product whose market has shifted
- Your current positioning is producing weak signals (long sales cycles, heavy discounting, customer confusion, or inconsistent messaging across teams)
- You want to evaluate whether an alternative market category might serve you better

**Do not treat this as a one-time exercise.** Positioning should be revisited when competitive alternatives change, when new customer segments emerge, or when the market category itself evolves. The components give you a repeatable process, not a one-and-done deliverable.

**Do not skip the proof step for value claims.** If you cannot back a value claim with data or third-party opinion, do not include it in your positioning. Unsubstantiated claims are worse than silence.

**Do not use a trend as a substitute for a market category.** Pairing 'AI-powered' with no market category declaration leaves customers unable to understand what your product actually is.

## Code Examples

The five (plus one) components expressed as a positioning canvas — fill this in sequentially, not as a single statement:

```
POSITIONING CANVAS — Five (Plus One) Components
================================================

1. COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVES
   What would best-fit customers use or do if our product did not exist?
   - [ ] Direct product competitors (list by name)
   - [ ] Non-product alternatives (spreadsheets, manual processes, do nothing)

2. UNIQUE ATTRIBUTES
   Features/capabilities we have that those alternatives lack:
   - [ ] Attribute A → unique vs. which alternative(s)?
   - [ ] Attribute B → unique vs. which alternative(s)?
   (Remove any attribute that is not unique relative to at least one key alternative)

3. VALUE (AND PROOF)
   What does each unique attribute enable for the customer?
   Attribute → Benefit → Value Theme
   - [ ] Attribute A → Benefit → Value
         Proof: [data source / customer quote / analyst opinion]
   - [ ] Attribute B → Benefit → Value
         Proof: [data source / customer quote / analyst opinion]
   Rule: the company's own opinion is NOT valid proof.

4. TARGET MARKET CHARACTERISTICS
   The specific characteristics that make a customer love this more than anyone else:
   - [ ] Must-have characteristic (not just firmographic — what do they value?)
   - [ ] Secondary signals (buying behavior, use case, role)

5. MARKET CATEGORY
   The frame of reference that puts our strengths in their best context:
   - [ ] Chosen category:
   - [ ] Assumptions it triggers: (list them — do they help or hurt?)
   - [ ] Alternative categories considered: (why rejected?)

6. RELEVANT TRENDS (OPTIONAL)
   Trends our target customers understand and care about right now:
   - [ ] Trend:
   - [ ] Why it makes our category more urgent today:
   Rule: trend must accompany a market category, never replace it.
```

The canvas makes explicit what the traditional positioning statement hides: you must do *discovery work* at each step before moving to the next. An answer that cannot survive scrutiny at step 2 (is this truly unique?) does not earn its way into step 3.
