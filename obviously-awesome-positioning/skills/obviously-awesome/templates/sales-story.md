# Sales Story Arc — Step 10 Output

> **Purpose:** Translate completed positioning (competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value themes, best-fit customers, market frame) into a seven-stage pitch arc your team aligns on before marketing writes any copy.
> **Driving question:** "How do we turn our positioning into a story a salesperson can tell on a first call?"

Fill the seven stages **in order**. Each stage is one fillable line; do not skip ahead or collapse stages.

## 1. Problem
The specific problem the product was designed to solve, and why it is painful for best-fit customers.

> <problem statement — one or two sentences; name why it hurts best-fit customers>

## 2. Current solutions and shortcomings
How best-fit customers attempt to solve the problem today (from competitive alternatives, Step 4), and where each falls short. List 2–3.

| Current solution | Key shortcoming |
|---|---|
| <current approach 1> | <where it falls short> |
| <current approach 2> | <where it falls short> |
| <current approach 3> | … |

## 3. The Perfect World
The criteria a prospect would use to evaluate an ideal solution — given the problem and the gaps above. Describe the criteria, not the product. List 3–5.

- [ ] <ideal-solution criterion 1>
- [ ] <ideal-solution criterion 2>
- [ ] <ideal-solution criterion 3>
- [ ] <ideal-solution criterion 4 — optional>
- [ ] <ideal-solution criterion 5 — optional>

## 4. Introduce the product (in its market category)
Introduce the product by name and category. State what it IS before what it does. Pattern: *"[Product] is a [market category] that…"*

> <Product> is a <market category> that <what it does>. *(Positioning style: <Head to Head | Big Fish Small Pond | Create a New Game>)*

## 5. Value themes
Carried from Step 6. For each theme: name it, then state which unique attributes enable it and how. Each theme needs ≥1 proof point. List 1–4.

| # | Value theme | Enabled by (unique attributes) | Proof point |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | <value theme> | <attribute(s) that enable it + how> | <proof point> |
| 2 | <value theme> | … | … |
| 3 | <value theme> | … | … |
| 4 | <value theme> | … | … |

## 6. Objections and proof
The 2–3 most common objections from best-fit customers, each with a reframe and a case study / representative customer to cite.

| Objection | Reframe | Proof / case study |
|---|---|---|
| <objection 1> | <one- or two-sentence reframe> | <customer case study or reference> |
| <objection 2> | … | … |
| <objection 3> | … | … |

## 7. Next action
The single, specific next step you want the prospect to take.

> <one call to action — e.g. schedule a demo, start a trial, meet a solutions engineer>

---

## Fill rules

- **All seven stages present and IN ORDER.** Do not reorder, merge, or skip a stage — the arc only works as a sequence.
- **No placeholders in the final.** Every `<...>` and `…` must be replaced with specific, agreed-upon content before the arc is considered done.
- **Stage 2: 2–3 current solutions, no more.** Draw them from the competitive alternatives list (Step 4); name a concrete shortcoming for each.
- **Stage 3 describes criteria, not the product.** The Perfect World lists what an ideal solution would do, tied directly to the gaps in Stage 2 — so Stage 4's introduction feels inevitable.
- **Stage 4: state what the product IS before what it does**, and use a market category that matches the chosen positioning style (Head to Head, Big Fish Small Pond, or Create a New Game).
- **Stage 5: 1–4 value themes, each with ≥1 proof point.** Do not collapse the arc into a feature list — name the value, then the enabling attributes.
- **Stage 6: 2–3 objections**, each with a reframe and a citable case study or customer.
- **Stage 7: exactly one call to action.** Make it easy for the prospect to say yes.
- **Team-review the whole arc before any marketing copy is written.** Per the book's rule, messaging is not drafted live with the group — marketing converts the agreed arc into a master messaging document afterward.
