## Fill rules

- Describe the segment with **IDENTIFIABLE behavioral and attitudinal characteristics** — behaviors, technology investments, skills gaps, selling motions, strategic priorities — that tie directly to the value you deliver.
- **Firmographics alone are the named anti-pattern.** Company size, industry, or geography by themselves ("small businesses," "100–2,000 employees") are not a segmentation — knowing a prospect employs 500 people says nothing about whether they care about what you do. Combine multiple identifiable characteristics; never replace firmographics with a single behavioral trait and call it done.
- Every characteristic must be (a) **identifiable before a sale** and (b) **directly tied to caring** about your specific value. The segment must be **findable and targetable** — a rep should be able to spot a member in the wild.
- Run the "who cares and why?" loop for **every value theme from Step 6**, then look for **overlap** — the characteristics that recur across themes define your best-fit profile.
- A segment is only worth focusing on if it passes **both** criteria: **large enough** to meet near-term sales goals AND has an **important, specific, unmet need common across all members**. If either fails, discard the segment or broaden the definition.
- **Narrow to win:** target as narrowly as you can to meet near-term sales objectives — narrow until the rules favor your strengths, but keep it big enough to matter. Only broaden if you cannot hit targets on best-fit alone, and broaden by adding identifiable characteristics, not by reverting to vague demographics.
- Mark the segmentation as **current-state only** — do not future-proof it. Record a review trigger.
