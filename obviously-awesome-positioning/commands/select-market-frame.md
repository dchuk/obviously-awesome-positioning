# Select Market Frame of Reference

**Purpose:** Walk through Step 8 of the positioning process to choose the single market frame of reference that puts your product's strengths at the center. Use this command after you have completed Steps 1–7 (best-fit customers, competitive alternatives, unique attributes, and value themes are already identified).

**When to use:**
- You have completed Steps 1–7 and are ready to name a market category
- You are repositioning an existing product and suspect the current category no longer fits
- You are unsure whether to compete head-on, own a subsegment, or define an entirely new category
- Adjacent markets are shifting and you want to evaluate whether your product belongs there

---

## Steps

1. **Gather inputs.** Read any positioning notes, pitch decks, or documents the user provides. Confirm you have:
   - A list of best-fit customers and the patterns in why they love the product
   - The ranked list of competitive alternatives (what customers would do without you)
   - The product's unique attributes and their mapped value themes
   - Any existing market category language the team currently uses

   If any of these are missing, ask the user to supply them before continuing. Refer to [identify-best-fit-customers.md](identify-best-fit-customers.md), [list-competitive-alternatives.md](list-competitive-alternatives.md), and [map-value-themes.md](map-value-themes.md) if earlier steps need to be completed first.

2. **Apply abductive reasoning.** List the product's key features and the value they deliver. Ask: what types of products typically have these features and deliver this value? Write out at least two candidate market categories that emerge from this exercise.

3. **Examine adjacent markets.** Identify markets neighboring the candidate categories. For each adjacency, assess:
   - Do the product's features and value look more at home there than in the original category?
   - Is the adjacent market growing quickly, bringing buyer attention, media focus, and buzz?
   - Would moving there be justified by genuine fit, or only by borrowed buzz?

   Flag any adjacency that passes the genuine-fit test as an additional candidate.

4. **Evaluate Head to Head.** For each candidate category, apply the following yes/no test:

   | Question | Answer |
   |---|---|
   | Is the category well understood by buyers? | yes / no |
   | Are you already the market leader in this category? | yes / no |
   | Is no clear leader yet established in the category? | yes / no |
   | Can you credibly demonstrate superiority on the currently accepted purchase criteria? | yes / no |
   | Do you have the features, customer proof points, and brand to compete on the leader's terms? | yes / no |

   **Head to Head is viable** if the category is understood AND (you are the leader OR no leader exists yet AND you can compete on the established criteria). Otherwise mark it as not viable.

5. **Evaluate Big Fish, Small Pond.** For the leading candidate category, apply:

   | Question | Answer |
   |---|---|
   | Is the broader category well-defined and understood by buyers? | yes / no |
   | Does a clear market leader exist? | yes / no |
   | Is there an identifiable subsegment with a distinct, important unmet need? | yes / no |
   | Can you construct a list of prospective buyers in the subsegment via research or data? | yes / no |
   | Is the subsegment large enough to meet near-term revenue goals? | yes / no |
   | Is your competitive advantage in this subsegment difficult for the leader to copy? | yes / no |

   **Big Fish, Small Pond is viable** only if all six answers are yes. If the category is not well understood, mark it not viable — subsegmenting an unclear category creates more confusion, not less.

6. **Evaluate Create a New Game.** Only proceed to this evaluation if no existing or adjacent category scored viable in steps 4–5. Apply:

   | Question | Answer |
   |---|---|
   | Have you evaluated every plausible existing category and none showcases your true differentiators? | yes / no |
   | Can you identify a massive enabling change (new technology, economic shift, regulation, behavior shift) that makes this category newly possible or necessary? | yes / no |
   | Can you answer 'Why now?' with compelling external market forces (not just internal ambition)? | yes / no |
   | Does your solution require both new category boundaries AND new purchase criteria simultaneously? | yes / no |
   | Do you have a large, well-resourced company or patient investors willing to fund years of market education? | yes / no |

   **Create a New Game is viable** if all five answers are yes. Warn the user that this style is the highest-cost option and should be used only as a last resort or when resources are substantial. See [market-positioning-styles.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/patterns/market-positioning-styles.md) for deeper guidance on all three styles.

7. **Select the single market frame.** Based on the evaluation tables, recommend exactly one market frame of reference. Present the recommendation in this format:

   ```
   Recommended style: [Head to Head | Big Fish, Small Pond | Create a New Game]
   Market frame of reference: [specific category or subsegment name]
   Rationale: [2–3 sentences grounding the choice in the evaluation results above]
   Disqualified alternatives: [list styles not chosen and the specific rule that disqualified each]
   ```

   If more than one style scored viable, recommend Big Fish, Small Pond over Head to Head when a clear leader exists, and always prefer an existing category over Create a New Game unless no existing category fits.

   Offer the user the blank template at `../skills/obviously-awesome/templates/market-frame.md` — populate it directly with the candidate categories, the three style evaluation tables, and the recommendation block, or hand it over for the user to fill in.

8. **Surface anti-patterns to avoid.** Based on the chosen style, flag any risks:
   - If the user is leaning toward Create a New Game without strong external forces, name the **Trend Without Market Declaration** anti-pattern: anchoring positioning to a trend rather than a defined market misleads buyers about what the product actually is and delays their purchase decision.
   - If the team is defaulting to the current category out of habit rather than deliberate evaluation, name the **feature of a big player** trap: positioning a genuinely differentiated product as a minor feature of a dominant platform destroys perceived value.

   See [positioning-anti-patterns.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/anti-patterns/positioning-anti-patterns.md) for the full catalog.

---

## Verify

The selection is complete when you can confirm all of the following:

- [ ] At least two candidate market categories were generated via abductive reasoning
- [ ] All three styles — Head to Head, Big Fish Small Pond, and Create a New Game — were evaluated, not just the intuitively obvious one
- [ ] The recommended market frame passes every yes/no rule for its style (no partial passes)
- [ ] Disqualified styles each map to a specific rule that eliminated them
- [ ] If Big Fish, Small Pond was chosen: the subsegment is named, listable, and sized against near-term revenue goals
- [ ] If Create a New Game was chosen: a 'Why now?' narrative based on external forces is articulated
- [ ] The output names the exact market frame of reference — not a vague direction like 'something in the data space'

## Notes

- **Positioning style selection is Step 8 of 10.** The output of this command feeds directly into Step 9 (optional trend layering) and Step 10 (the sales story arc). See [trend-layering.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/topics/trend-layering.md) and [build-sales-story.md](build-sales-story.md).
- **Customer input is useful but not authoritative.** Customers default to familiar categories tied to their industry. Treat their category language as a data point to inform abductive reasoning, not as the answer.
- **Adjacent markets are legitimate candidates.** If lines between markets are shifting due to new technology or new entrants, and your product's value is more at home in an adjacent space, repositioning there is a valid choice — provided the move is driven by genuine fit, not trend-chasing.
- **Re-evaluate periodically.** Competitive landscape changes, new entrants, or product evolution can invalidate a previously correct market frame. Use [review-positioning-health.md](review-positioning-health.md) to run a periodic check.
- For foundational context on what a market frame of reference is and why it matters, see [positioning-foundations.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/core/positioning-foundations.md) and [five-plus-one-components.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/core/five-plus-one-components.md).
