# Build Sales Story

**Purpose:** Translate your completed positioning into a structured sales story arc your team can use on first calls. Use this command immediately after finishing the 10-step positioning process with your full cross-functional team.

**When to use:**
- You have completed Steps 1–10 of the positioning process and have agreed-upon competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value themes, best-fit customers, and a market frame of reference
- Your team needs to align on a single pitch narrative before marketing builds copy
- A new sales rep or partner needs to learn how to position the product in conversation
- You have repositioned and need to rebuild the pitch arc from scratch

---

## Steps

1. **Gather positioning inputs.** Read or ask the user to provide the outputs of the positioning exercise:
   - Competitive alternatives (Step 4)
   - Unique attributes (Step 5)
   - Value themes (Step 6)
   - Best-fit customer segments (Step 7)
   - Market frame of reference (Step 8)

   If any of these are missing, stop and direct the user to complete the relevant command first: [list-competitive-alternatives.md](list-competitive-alternatives.md), [map-value-themes.md](map-value-themes.md), or [select-market-frame.md](select-market-frame.md).

2. **Arc Step 1 — Define the problem.** Ask the user: "What is the specific problem your product was designed to solve?" Push for a one- or two-sentence framing that sets the boundary of the conversation for prospects. Record the agreed-upon problem statement.

3. **Arc Step 2 — Describe current solutions and shortcomings.** Using the competitive alternatives list from Step 4 of the positioning process, ask: "How are best-fit customers currently attempting to solve this problem?" Then ask: "Where does each of those alternatives fall short?" Produce a brief list of current approaches paired with their key gaps.

4. **Arc Step 3 — Paint the perfect world.** Ask the user: "Given the problem and the gaps in current solutions, what would the features of an ideal solution look like?" This is **The Perfect World** stage — describe the criteria a prospect would use to evaluate a perfect solution, not the product itself. Record 3–5 ideal-solution criteria.

5. **Arc Step 4 — Introduce the product in its market category.** Using the market frame of reference output, introduce the product by name and category. Example pattern: *"[Product] is a [market category] that…"* Confirm the category label matches the chosen positioning style (Head to Head, Big Fish Small Pond, or Create a New Game — see [market-positioning-styles.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/patterns/market-positioning-styles.md)).

6. **Arc Step 5 — Detail each value theme.** For each value theme identified in Step 6 of the positioning process, produce a two-part statement: (a) name the value theme, and (b) explain which unique attributes enable that value and how. Limit to 1–4 value themes. See [value-themes.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/topics/value-themes.md) for the features→benefits→value chain.

7. **Arc Step 6 — Handle objections and add proof.** Ask the user: "What are the most common objections prospects raise?" For each objection, draft a one- or two-sentence reframe. Then ask for a customer case study or a representative list of current customers that can be cited at this stage of the call.

8. **Arc Step 7 — State the desired next action.** Ask: "What do you want the prospect to do at the end of this conversation?" (e.g., schedule a demo, start a trial, meet a solutions engineer). Record a single, specific call to action.

9. **Assemble and review the full arc.** Output the complete story arc as a numbered list using the inputs collected above. Present it to the user and ask: "Does the team agree on the problem definition, the gaps in current solutions, the ideal-solution criteria, and the key purchase criteria implied by the value themes?" Iterate on any section where agreement is missing.

---

## Verify

The command succeeds when:

- [ ] All 7 arc steps are populated with specific, agreed-upon content — no placeholders
- [ ] The problem statement in Arc Step 1 is consistent with the competitive alternatives named in Arc Step 2
- [ ] The perfect-world criteria in Arc Step 3 map directly to the value themes in Arc Step 5 (a prospect who accepts the criteria will find the product compelling)
- [ ] The market category in Arc Step 4 matches the positioning style chosen in the positioning process
- [ ] The full team has reviewed and verbally confirmed agreement on the arc before anyone writes copy

If any arc step is missing or the team cannot agree, flag the specific disagreement and suggest returning to the relevant positioning step rather than forcing a premature close.

---

## Notes

- **Do not write marketing copy during this session.** Per the book's rule (`ch14.rule.messaging-not-in-group`), messaging should not be written in real time with a group. The marketing team converts the agreed sales story arc into a master messaging document independently afterward.
- The sales story arc is an input to the messaging document, not a substitute for it. See [sales-story-and-messaging.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/topics/sales-story-and-messaging.md) for the full implementation path.
- If your competitive landscape has changed since the positioning exercise, the arc built here may be stale. Run [review-positioning-health.md](review-positioning-health.md) first.
- Positioning is a business strategy shift, not a marketing exercise — ensure leadership is aligned before the team starts building campaigns from this arc. See [positioning-foundations.md](../skills/obviously-awesome/references/core/positioning-foundations.md) for the strategic framing.
